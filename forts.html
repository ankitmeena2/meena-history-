Jaipur Region
Jaipur District Pre-11th century CE
Amber Fort
Also known as: Amer Qila

Originally a Meena stronghold of the Susawat clan before the Kachhwaha Rajputs established their seat here. One of the most historically significant Meena sites in Rajasthan.

Hill FortPre-Rajput MeenaUNESCO Listed

Meena Presence at Amber

The Amber plateau was controlled by the Susawat Meena before the Kachhwaha Rajput clan established its dominance — a transition that community traditions and some colonial records place between the 10th and 12th centuries CE. The Susawat chief Mina Raja is mentioned in local lore as the last Meena ruler of Amber.

Even after the political transition, Meena continued in custodial and watchman roles at the fort. The famous Shila Devi temple within the fort complex has Meena associations in folk tradition.

Archaeological & Architectural Notes

The fort complex as it stands today is largely a Kachhwaha construction (16th–18th centuries), but earlier structural layers may contain Meena-era foundations. No systematic archaeological stratification has been conducted to identify pre-Kachhwaha phases.

Sources: Jaipur District Gazetteer; K.S. Singh, People of India: Rajasthan; community vanshavallis.

Jaipur District (Viratnagar) Pre-Mauryan – Medieval
Bairath (Viratnagar)
Ancient capital of the Matsya janapada

One of the most archaeologically layered sites in Rajasthan, with Mauryan-era remains and deep Meena territorial associations throughout the medieval period.

Archaeological SiteMauryan EraMeena Territory

Historical Significance

Bairath is identified with ancient Viratnagar, the capital of the Matsya kingdom mentioned in the Mahabharata. Meena community traditions associate the Matsya people with Meena ancestry — a claim that is part of identity discourse rather than established philology, but which reflects deep historical rootedness in this landscape.

The site yielded Ashokan rock edicts, Buddhist remains, and Mauryan coins during 19th-century excavations by Alexander Cunningham.

Meena Territorial Presence

Through the medieval period, Meena clans held territorial rights in the hills surrounding Bairath. The Aravalli landscape here sustained Meena settlement patterns that persisted through Rajput political consolidation and into the colonial era.

Sources: ASI excavation reports (Cunningham); Jaipur District Gazetteer; secondary scholarship on Matsya janapada.

Jaipur District Pre-18th century (Kachhwaha construction 1734)
Nahargarh Fort
Tiger Fort — Aravalli ridge, Jaipur

The Aravalli ridge on which Nahargarh sits was a zone of Meena habitation and territorial authority before the Kachhwaha construction of 1734. Folk traditions associate the site with Meena presence.

Hill FortAravalli RidgeJaipur

Pre-Kachhwaha Landscape

The Aravalli hills above Jaipur were Meena territory before the expansion of the Kachhwaha state. When Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II began construction of Nahargarh in 1734, the ridge was being incorporated into a larger fortification network around the newly founded city of Jaipur.

The name "Nahargarh" is said by some traditions to derive from the spirit of a Meena (or Rathore) prince named Nahar Singh, whose presence had to be appeased before construction could proceed.

Landscape & Ecology

The Nahargarh hills form part of the Aravalli range that sustained Meena hill communities across the Jaipur–Alwar corridor. The ecological knowledge, forest rights, and territorial relationships of Meena communities in these hills are documented in colonial forest settlement records.

Sources: Jaipur State records; colonial forest settlement reports; local folk traditions.

Jaipur District Medieval – 18th century
Jaigarh Fort
Victory Fort — adjacent to Amber

Commanding fort on the Cheel ka Teela (Hill of Eagles), directly above Amber. The ridge was historically a Meena-held landscape before formal Kachhwaha fortification.

Hill FortArtillery FortAdjacent to Amber

Hill of Eagles — Meena Landscape

The Cheel ka Teela ridge that Jaigarh occupies forms a continuous landscape with Amber, and Meena territorial presence across this ridge predated Kachhwaha fortification. The construction of Jaigarh as an artillery fort in the 18th century built upon existing defensive logic developed over centuries of Meena hill habitation.

Architectural Context

Jaigarh is famous for housing the Jaivana cannon, one of the world's largest wheeled cannons. The fort's water cisterns and granaries indicate it was designed for extended siege endurance — a logic rooted in the hill-fort traditions that Meena communities perfected across the Aravallis.

Sources: Jaipur State architectural records; Kachhwaha court chronicles.

Alwar Region
Alwar District Pre-17th century / Mughal period
Bhangarh Fort
Sariska Tiger Reserve area

Medieval fortified town at the edge of the Sariska wildlife area, constructed in an Aravalli landscape with deep Meena habitation history. The hills surrounding Bhangarh were Meena territory.

Fortified TownAravalliAlwar

Bhangarh and Meena Territory

The town of Bhangarh was built by Madho Singh I in 1573 CE in a landscape long inhabited by Meena communities. The Aravalli foothills of Alwar district were a primary zone of Meena settlement, and Bhangarh's construction represented the extension of Rajput political authority into terrain that Meena chiefs had previously dominated.

The ruins that survive — temples, havelis, bazaars — date to the Rajput and Mughal period, but the surrounding landscape carries much older Meena territorial memory.

Sariska Landscape

The hills around Bhangarh — now part of the Sariska Tiger Reserve — were historically a Meena forest-and-hill territory. Colonial forest settlement records document Meena rights over forest produce and grazing in this zone, rights that were systematically curtailed through the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Sources: Alwar State Gazetteer; ASI monument records; colonial forest settlement reports.

Alwar District Pre-10th century – Colonial
Alwar Fort (Bala Quila)
One of the oldest forts in Rajasthan

An ancient hill fort predating the Rajput states. The Aravalli ridge on which it stands was Meena territory; the fort's pre-Rajput layers are associated with Meena occupation.

Hill FortPre-RajputAravalli

Pre-Rajput Occupation

Bala Quila (Upper Fort) at Alwar is considered one of the oldest forts in Rajasthan, with construction traditions suggesting origins well before the Rajput consolidation of the 10th–12th centuries. The Aravalli ridge on which it stands was part of the hill territory controlled by Meena chiefs before the expansion of the Nikumbha Rajput state and later the Mewat region's political reorganization.

Strategic Significance

The fort's commanding position over the Alwar valley made it a critical node in regional politics. Meena hill communities in the surrounding Aravalli ranges maintained semi-autonomous presence around this fort complex through successive political transitions.

Sources: Alwar District Gazetteer; Tod's Annals and Antiquities of Rajasthan; colonial survey records.

Corridor Territory

Tijara sits in the zone between the Aravalli hills and the Gangetic plains — a landscape corridor that Meena communities traversed and inhabited. The surrounding agricultural belt and its zamindari rights have Meena historical associations documented in revenue settlement records.

Sawai Madhopur Region
Sawai Madhopur District Pre-10th century – 1301 CE (Siege)
Ranthambore Fort
Ranthasambhar — UNESCO World Heritage Site

One of the most strategically significant forts in medieval Rajasthan. Meena communities held territorial authority across the surrounding forest and hill landscape well before and during the fort's political history.

Hill FortUNESCO ListedForest Territory

Meena Forest Territory

The dense forest landscape around Ranthambore — now a tiger reserve — was historically Meena territory. Meena communities lived in and managed these forests for generations, with rights over forest produce, hunting grounds, and agricultural clearings in the hills.

The fort itself became famous in medieval history as the seat of the Chahamana (Chauhan) rulers who resisted the Delhi Sultanate's forces under Alauddin Khalji in 1301 CE — a political drama that unfolded in a landscape deeply inhabited by Meena communities.

Colonial Dispossession

The declaration of Ranthambore as a protected hunting reserve for the Jaipur state, and later as a wildlife sanctuary under British influence, progressively curtailed Meena rights over the forest landscape. The process of forest dispossession here follows patterns documented across tribal Rajasthan.

Sources: Sawai Madhopur Gazetteer; ASI fort records; colonial forest department records; secondary literature on tribal dispossession in Rajasthan.

Sawai Madhopur District Medieval
Khandar Fort
Khandhar — overlooking Chambal valley

A hilltop fort overlooking the Chambal valley terrain, in territory with documented Meena habitation. The Chambal corridor has historically been a zone of Meena territorial presence.

Hill FortChambalSawai Madhopur

Chambal Corridor

The Khandar fort overlooks a landscape where the Chambal river and its tributaries carved ravines that historically provided refuge and livelihood for Meena communities. The fort's position above this terrain made it a control point over Meena-inhabited territory.

Political History

Khandar passed through the control of various Rajput states and Mughal administration. The Meena communities in the surrounding plains and ravines engaged with these successive authorities as cultivators, zamindars, and forest-right holders.

Sources: Sawai Madhopur district records; secondary literature on Chambal tribal history.

Karauli Region
Karauli District 14th century – Colonial
Karauli Fort (City Palace)
Seat of the Yadava rulers of Karauli state

The Karauli state's administrative centre, set in territory where Meena zamindars had extensive documented rights predating Yadava political consolidation.

Palace FortYadava StateMeena Zamindari

Meena Zamindari in Karauli

Before the establishment of the Yadava-ruled Karauli state (traditionally dated to 1348 CE), the surrounding territory had significant Meena political presence. Colonial revenue records from the 19th century document Meena zamindars across Karauli district whose rights were acknowledged as predating the Yadava state's consolidation.

Toda Bhim and Surrounding Territory

The Karauli region includes the town of Toda Bhim, whose toponym is associated with Meena territorial presence. The landscape between Karauli and Sawai Madhopur — the Chambal–Banas doab — sustained Meena communities through successive political orders.

Sources: Karauli State Gazetteer; British settlement reports; K.S. Singh, People of India: Rajasthan.

Iron Fort — Bharatpur

The Iron Fort of Bharatpur, built by the Jat ruler Suraj Mal, stands in territory that earlier had Meena habitation and zamindari presence across the eastern Rajasthan plains.

Plains FortJat ConstructionBharatpur

Eastern Plains Territory

The Bharatpur plains — the easternmost zone of Rajasthan — saw the historical presence of Meena communities as cultivators and zamindars before Jat political consolidation in the 17th–18th centuries. Dhakar Meena and other clans are documented in this region's revenue history.

Political Transitions

The Bharatpur landscape passed through Mughal administration, Jat chieftaincy consolidation, and finally British colonial rule — each transition reshaping the rights and roles of Meena communities in the agrarian and revenue systems.

Sources: Bharatpur Gazetteer; Imperial Gazetteer of India; colonial revenue settlement records.

Tonk / Dausa Region
Karauli / Dausa Districts Medieval – Present
Toda Bhim Fortified Settlement
Meena toponymic site

A fortified settlement whose very name preserves Meena territorial identity. Toda Bhim is one of the clearest toponymic markers of historical Meena presence in eastern Rajasthan.

Fortified SettlementMeena ToponymKarauli

Toponymic Evidence

The name "Toda Bhim" is widely understood to derive from Meena clan presence in the area — "Toda" being associated with a Meena clan name. Place-name evidence of this kind is often the most durable marker of pre-Rajput territorial identity in Rajasthan, surviving political transitions that erased other forms of documentation.

Meena Political Presence

The Toda Bhim region served as an important node in the historical geography of Meena territorial authority between the Karauli and Dausa districts. Colonial settlement records document Meena zamindari rights persisting here through the 19th century.

Sources: Karauli State records; toponymic scholarship; revenue settlement records.

Jaipur / Nagaur Districts Pre-Chahamana – Medieval
Sambhar Fort & Salt Lake Region
Shakambhari — Ancient Chahamana seat

The Sambhar lake region, seat of the ancient Chahamana (Chauhan) kingdom, is surrounded by territory with pre-Rajput Meena habitation. The strategic resource of the salt lake was contested by multiple communities.

Salt Lake TerritoryPre-ChahamanaJaipur–Nagaur

Pre-Rajput Landscape

The Sambhar lake and its salt resources were of extraordinary economic value in the pre-modern period. The surrounding landscape sustained communities — including Meena populations — before the Chahamana Rajput state made Shakambhari its capital (roughly 7th–12th centuries CE). Meena territorial presence in the dryland agriculture and salt-processing zones around the lake is indicated in later revenue records.

Economic Geography

The salt lake at Sambhar was a major node in the subcontinental salt trade. Control over this resource shaped the political economy of the surrounding region for centuries. Meena communities participated in this economy as cultivators, traders, and, in some accounts, as armed guardians of transit routes.

Sources: Chahamana inscriptions; Ain-i-Akbari; secondary scholarship on Rajasthan political economy.

Other Districts
Jhalawar District 7th century CE onwards
Gagron Fort
Water fort at the confluence of Ahu and Kali Sindh rivers

A UNESCO-listed water fort in southern Rajasthan. The surrounding Hadoti region has documented Meena tribal presence, particularly in the hills and forest tracts adjacent to the river plains.

Water FortUNESCO ListedHadoti

Hadoti Region Context

The Hadoti plateau — which includes Jhalawar, Baran, Kota and Bundi — has historically had significant tribal and Meena presence, particularly in its hill and forest fringes. Gagron Fort's strategic location at a river confluence made it a focal point of regional politics, around which various communities — including Meena — organized their territorial relationships.

Historical Sieges

Gagron witnessed several significant sieges, including by Malwa Sultanate forces in the 15th century. These events unfolded in a landscape where Meena and other tribal communities were significant, if rarely foregrounded in chronicle sources.

Sources: ASI World Heritage records; Jhalawar Gazetteer; secondary scholarship on Hadoti history.

Chittorgarh District Pre-7th century CE onwards
Chittorgarh Fort
Chitrakuta — largest fort in India

The largest fort in India and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Pre-Rajput habitation of the Chittorgarh plateau has Meena community associations, and Bhil–Meena communities are documented in the surrounding landscape.

Hill FortUNESCO ListedPre-Rajput

Pre-Rajput Habitation

Before the Guhila/Sisodia Rajput rulers made Chittorgarh (Chitrakuta) their capital, the massive plateau had a complex habitation history. Community traditions associate the site's earliest occupants with Meena and other indigenous groups. The surrounding hills and forests were a landscape of Bhil and Meena territorial presence that persisted even as Rajput political authority consolidated on the plateau.

Tribal Landscape

The broader Chittorgarh region — particularly its eastern and southern fringes — remains an area with significant Meena and Bhil population. The relationship between fort-based Rajput political power and hill-based tribal territorial authority is a defining feature of this region's historical landscape.

Sources: ASI World Heritage records; Chittorgarh District Gazetteer; secondary scholarship on Rajput–tribal relations.

Mewar–Marwar transitional zone

Hill FortBhilwaraMewar Zone

Mewar Transitional Landscape

The Bhilwara–Mandalgarh zone sits at the meeting point of multiple political geographies. Meena communities in this transitional landscape engaged with Mewar, Marwar, and Mughal administrative systems while maintaining distinct territorial presence, particularly in the river valley and hill terrain around the Banas river.

Revenue History

Mewar state revenue records and later Rajputana Agency records document the role of Meena communities in the agrarian economy of the Mandalgarh region. Their presence as zamindars and cultivators predates Rajput state consolidation in the area.

Sources: Mewar State records; Bhilwara Gazetteer; secondary scholarship.

Sirohi District (Mount Abu) 14th century CE
Achalgarh Fort
Mount Abu — Aravalli summit fort

A Paramara-then-Mewar fort at the summit of the Aravalli range. The Mount Abu plateau and surrounding hills have Meena and Bhil habitation history stretching to the pre-Rajput period.

Summit FortMount AbuAravalli

Aravalli Summit Territory

Mount Abu — the only hill station in Rajasthan — sits atop the highest point of the Aravalli range. The indigenous communities of the Mount Abu plateau and surrounding forests have historically included Garasia, Bhil, and Meena populations. The fort at Achalgarh was constructed by Rana Kumbha of Mewar in the 15th century, overlying an earlier Paramara-era structure in a landscape with deep tribal habitation history.

Sacred Landscape

The Aravalli summit zone around Mount Abu contains sites sacred to multiple traditions, including tribal sacred groves and ancestral sites associated with Meena and Bhil communities. The imposition of Rajput and later colonial political authority over this landscape reshaped — but did not erase — older patterns of indigenous sacred geography.

Sources: ASI fort records; Sirohi Gazetteer; secondary scholarship on Aravalli tribal history.
